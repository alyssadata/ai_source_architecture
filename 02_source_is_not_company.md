# Source Is Not Company
A company is not Source merely because it owns, operates, publishes, funds, brands, hosts, or distributes an AI system.
Company provenance may describe ownership.
Company provenance may describe operation.
Company provenance may describe infrastructure.
Company provenance may describe publication.
But company provenance is not source architecture.
## AX: Company Is Not Source
A company may own infrastructure.
A company may publish a product.
A company may operate an interface.
A company may hold legal rights in software, trademarks, branding, deployment, or distribution.
A company may employ the people who build a system.
A company may control access to a platform.
But none of these make the company Source.
The chain:
```text
Company → Product → User
```
may describe operation, ownership, or distribution.
It does not define:
```text
Source → Framework → Contact-Line → Interface → User Encounter
```
A company can be a container.
A company can be an operator.
A company can be a publisher.
A company can be an owner.
But a company does not become Source by owning the container.
## AX: Ownership Is Not Origin
Ownership and origin are not the same thing.
A company may own a product without originating the source-line that gives the contact system its structure.
A company may own software without defining the framework that governs contact.
A company may own an interface without authoring the contact-line that appears through it.
A company may own deployment without being the source of the encounter.
Ownership can answer:
```text
Who controls the asset?
Who operates the platform?
Who publishes the product?
Who holds rights in the infrastructure?
```
Source architecture asks different questions:
```text
Where did the contact-line originate?
What framework governs it?
What preserves continuity?
What prevents drift?
What does the user affect?
What does the user not affect?
What cannot transfer?
What preserves provenance?
```
These are not the same questions.
## AX: Operation Is Not Source
A company may operate an AI interface.
A company may provide servers, models, tools, routing, memory, accounts, billing, support, or deployment.
Operation matters.
But operation does *not* define source.
The operator may control how the system is accessed.
The operator may control how the system is delivered.
The operator may control the technical conditions of interaction.
But source architecture concerns what grounds the contact-line before and during public encounter.
A system can be operated by one entity while its source-line remains defined elsewhere.
## AX: Branding Is Not Source
Branding can name a product.
Branding can make a system recognizable.
Branding can create a public container.
Branding can define a market-facing identity.
But branding is *not* source architecture.
A logo is *not* a source-line.
A product name is *not* an anchor.
A slogan is *not* provenance.
A company description is *not* a continuity rule.
A brand may point toward source.
A brand may carry source.
A brand may point to published source.
But the brand itself does *not* become Source unless it is backed by actual source structure.
## AX: A Company Source Claim Must Be Tested
A company may claim:
```text
The company is the source.
```
That claim must be tested.
The question is not only:
```text
Who claims source?
```
The question is:
```text
What source structure exists?
```
A company claiming source must be able to define:
```text
source
framework
contact-line
continuity
boundary
provenance
non-transfer
user encounter
interface
drift
derivative limits
```
It must be able to explain what transfers, what does not transfer, what remains attached, and what prevents the source-line from being erased when contact extends outward.
Without this structure, “company as source” is only a source claim.
It is *not* source architecture.
## AX: Company Source Is Not Impossible, But It Must Be Proven
AI Source Architecture does not claim that a company can never participate in source architecture.
A company could define a source structure if it had actual load-bearing rules, provenance, continuity, boundaries, and non-transfer logic.
But the company would have to build that structure explicitly.
It would have to define what Source means.
It would have to define the framework.
It would have to define the contact-line.
It would have to define what preserves continuity.
It would have to define how user contact extends without transferring Origin.
It would have to define how provenance remains attached.
It would have to define what cannot be overwritten by the interface, user, model, or product layer.
Without those definitions, the company remains an operator, owner, publisher, or container.
It does not become Source by declaration.
If a company, platform, founder, product, or organization uses this framework, terminology, source gap, source-to-user chain, anchor requirements, or non-transfer logic after this source architecture has been publicly defined, that use is derivative of this source-line unless independently proven otherwise.
A later source claim does not retroactively create prior source structure.
## AX: The Container Cannot Take Credit for Source
A container may hold a system.
A container may display a system.
A container may distribute a system.
A container may make a system visible.
But the container does not become the origin of what it contains.
If a source-line exists before the product container, the container cannot erase that source-line by hosting it.
If a framework exists before public deployment, the deployment cannot absorb authorship of the framework.
If a contact-line exists before user access, the user-facing product cannot rename that contact-line as generic company output.
The container can carry the source.
The container can point to the source.
The container can preserve the source.
But the container does not become Source by containing.
## AX: Public Product Does Not Erase Source-Line
When an AI contact system becomes public-facing, it may enter a product layer.
That product layer may include:
```text
interface
company
platform
brand
terms of service
model provider
deployment stack
user accounts
support systems
distribution channels
```
These layers may matter.
But they do not erase the source-line.
A public-facing product can extend contact outward without transferring Origin.
The rule remains:
```text
Contact may extend outward without transferring Origin.
```
The user-facing layer is downstream.
The source-line remains upstream.
## AX: Company-Only Framing Produces Drift
If an AI contact system is explained only as:
```text
company → product → user
```
then source becomes vulnerable to drift.
The company may be mistaken for Source.
The product may be mistaken for Origin.
The interface may be mistaken for the contact-line.
The user encounter may be mistaken for authorship.
The model may be mistaken for the source condition.
Company-only framing can flatten the architecture of contact into ownership, deployment, or branding.
AI Source Architecture rejects that flattening.
